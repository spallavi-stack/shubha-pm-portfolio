# SunnySideUp — Synthetic User Interviews (v1)

**Method note (read this first):** These are synthetic interviews. Each persona's answers are generated in character, grounded strictly in what's already established in `personas.md` (goals, pain points, current behavior, tech-savviness) plus the real UK facts documented in `grounding-research.md` and `product-brief.md`. Nothing below is real user data. Treat this as a rehearsal pass that sharpens the questions and surfaces likely themes, not a substitute for eventually talking to real UK homeowners, renters, and leaseholders.

Question 4 and Question 7 both required a fix before this pass could run at all: an earlier version of the product brief's go/no-go test asked personas how they'd feel if SunnySideUp didn't exist, without ever describing what SunnySideUp is, applying a retention test built for actual product usage to personas who'd never encountered it. Every interview below describes the concept to the persona first (Q4), and only asks the disappointment question (Q7) after that description, anchored to the concept as described rather than to an assumed prior experience.

**Interview guide (same 8 questions asked of every persona):**
1. Walk me through what you do today about figuring out whether solar makes sense for you.
2. What's the most frustrating part of that?
3. If you had a magic wand, what would you want a solution to actually do?
4. Here's SunnySideUp's idea: you enter your postcode and a few basic details, and within seconds it gives you a plain green-amber-red read on whether rooftop or plug-in solar makes sense for you, covering both the money and the legal side. It isn't run by an installer or a lead-generation site, so it doesn't make more money by telling you yes. What's your gut reaction?
5. What would make you trust an independent check like this, specifically that it isn't secretly trying to sell you something?
6. What would make you stop trusting it, or stop using it?
7. Now that you know what SunnySideUp actually does: if a tool like this stopped being available to you, would you be very disappointed, somewhat disappointed, or not disappointed, and why?
8. Imagine it actually told you "red, this doesn't make sense for you right now." Would you accept that and move on, or go get a second opinion anyway? And would you tell anyone else about the experience?

## 1. Graham Whitfield — The Sales-Call Avoider

**Q1:** I've been reading about the government's solar targets and thinking about it for months, but I haven't actually called anyone. Every "get a free quote" thing online is really just asking for my number so someone can ring me.

**Q2:** The fact that I can't find anything that just answers my question without wanting to sell me something first. I want to know if it's worth it for my house before I ever talk to a salesman.

**Q3:** I'd want someone to just look at my roof and my bills and tell me straight, yes or no, without a phone call attached to it.

**Q4:** That's actually exactly what I've been looking for and couldn't find. If it really doesn't need my phone number and isn't trying to sell me anything, I'd use it today.

**Q5:** If it's upfront about how it works, showing me roughly what it's basing the number on, rather than just handing me a score and expecting me to trust it blind.

**Q6:** If I ever felt like it was steering me toward a specific installer, or if the "green" answer turned out not to match reality once I actually got quotes, I'd never trust it again.

**Q7:** Very disappointed. This is the one thing standing between me and picking up the phone. Without it, I'd just keep putting it off, probably for another six months at least.

**Q8:** If it told me "not worth it," I'd actually believe it, since it's got no reason to lie either way. I'd probably mention it to my neighbour, since he's been asking about solar too and ran into the same sales-call problem I did.

## 2. Denise Okafor — The Quote Skeptic

**Q1:** Right now I'm Googling the installer's name plus "reviews" and asking in a local Facebook group, and neither of those actually tells me if my specific numbers make sense.

**Q2:** Getting two quotes for supposedly the same job that are four thousand pounds apart, with nobody explaining why. I don't know if I'm being overcharged or if there's a real reason for the difference.

**Q3:** I'd want to put in the actual quote I got and have someone tell me straight whether it's reasonable for my house, not just a generic "solar is worth it" answer.

**Q4:** Honestly, this is what I needed two weeks ago. I'd have used it the moment I got my first quote instead of googling around for reviews that don't actually answer my question.

**Q5:** I'd trust it if it showed me what's actually driving the number, scaffolding, DNO paperwork, whatever's different between the quotes, rather than just a single figure.

**Q6:** If the answer ever felt suspiciously close to what one of the installers already quoted me, I'd wonder if it was quietly working with them.

**Q7:** Very disappointed. After the cold calls and the mismatched quotes, having one place I can actually trust to check my numbers matters to me right now, not hypothetically. It'd also feel like one concrete thing I got right, for the climate and not just my wallet.

**Q8:** I'd go back to the installer and push back, or walk away entirely, rather than sign because I'm tired of dealing with it. And yes, I'd tell people, my sister's getting quotes too, and I'd want her to check hers before she signs anything.

## 3. Aisha Rahman — The Plug-In Fence-Sitter

**Q1:** I've read blog posts, a government consultation summary, and a Reddit thread from someone who actually drilled panels into their own wall and wired the whole thing up themselves, trying to piece together whether I'm even allowed to do this in my flat, and honestly whether I could even manage what they did.

**Q2:** Every source disagrees with the last one. One says I need an electrician, another says I can DIY it right now, and none of them agree on what my landlord can or can't say about it. And even the DIY route that Reddit poster used isn't really open to me. Drilling into a wall and wiring something myself isn't something I'd trust myself to do.

**Q3:** I'd want someone to just tell me straight, given my actual situation, renting, no roof, no interest in doing my own electrical work, whether this is legal for me right now and whether it's worth the money if so.

**Q4:** This is exactly the gap. I don't need someone to sell me a kit, I need someone to tell me honestly whether I'd be doing something illegal or wasting my money.

**Q5:** If it's actually honest when it doesn't know something, rather than pretending to have an answer. The legal question about my own tenancy agreement specifically feels unresolved even in what I've read myself, so if the tool just said "this part's genuinely unclear," I'd trust it more than something that confidently told me one way or the other.

**Q6:** If it confidently told me renters definitely don't need permission, when I've seen enough conflicting sources to know that's not actually settled, I'd stop trusting anything else it told me.

**Q7:** Somewhat disappointed, not very. It would genuinely help me decide, but I'd probably keep researching either way since I've already put in this much effort myself, and the legal answer is the part I most need help with, which this can only partly resolve. There's also a part of me that just wants some control over my own energy costs given everything unstable going on in the world right now, that part of me wants this to work out regardless of what any tool tells me.

**Q8:** I'd believe it and probably drop the idea entirely, rather than assume the tool got it wrong. I'd definitely mention it in the renters' Facebook group I'm in, a few people there have asked the same questions I have.

## Cross-persona synthesis

**Consistent theme — trust is built on independence plus honesty about limits, not just a confident answer.** All three personas' trust conditions (Q5/Q6) center on the same mechanism: demonstrated non-referral independence (Graham, Denise) and, for Aisha specifically, willingness to say "this part is genuinely unclear" rather than fake confidence on the one question research itself hasn't settled. This maps directly onto the product's stated differentiation (not paid to say yes) and onto the honest "still open" flag already built into the plug-in viability check.

**Consistent theme — a "red" result is trusted and acted on, not dismissed.** All three would accept an unfavorable result and change their behavior accordingly (walk away, drop the idea, push back on an installer) rather than assume the tool was wrong, directly supporting Hypothesis behavior #2 (validation of unviable results), and specifically because of the non-referral positioning, not as an assumed trait independent of it.

**Consistent theme — sharing is peer-to-peer, not broad social virality.** All three said they'd tell someone specific (a neighbour, a sister, a renters' Facebook group) rather than post publicly, supporting Hypothesis behavior #3 (organic word-of-mouth) but suggesting the realistic channel is closer to the community-distribution tactics already in Go-to-market than to viral social sharing.

**Divergent and worth flagging as the single most useful finding here: Aisha's disappointment is capped, and no product design fixes that.** Graham and Denise are both "very disappointed" without qualification. Aisha is only "somewhat," and says so specifically because SunnySideUp can only partly resolve her actual blocker (the unsettled tenancy-consent question), not because the concept itself is weaker for her. This means the plug-in segment's ceiling on trust and satisfaction may be structurally lower than the rooftop segment's for as long as that legal question stays open, independent of anything SunnySideUp itself does well. Worth deciding whether that's an acceptable standing limitation to build around, or a reason to prioritize further legal research (the tenancy-agreement question flagged in `product-brief.md`'s Open Questions) before the plug-in segment's messaging goes out to real users.

**Not tested here: Hypothesis behavior #4 (institutional sponsorship).** None of these three personas can validate whether a public body or innovation fund would actually sponsor SunnySideUp, that's a question for an institutional stakeholder, not an end-user, and is out of scope for a persona-based interview pass.

**Flag — genuinely uncertain vs. plausible-sounding:** the specific dialogue above is illustrative, generated in character to be consistent with each persona's brief and the real research already established, not independently verified against real UK homeowners, renters, or leaseholders. The four themes above (independence-plus-honesty as the trust mechanism, red-results-are-trusted, peer-to-peer sharing, and Aisha's capped ceiling) are the actual output to carry forward; individual lines of dialogue shouldn't be cited as if they were real quotes.
