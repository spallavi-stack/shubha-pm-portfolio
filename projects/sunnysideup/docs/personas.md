# SunnySideUp — Personas (v1, draft)

Grounded in `grounding-research.md`: UK solar sales practices and pricing opacity (CMA 2022 investigation, Which? cold-calling survey), the plug-in solar regulatory timeline (BS 7671 Amendment 4, SI 2026/848), the Renters' Rights Act 2025 finding, and the climate-concern/felt-ability-to-act gap.

Template per persona: identity, quote, goal, pain point, current behavior, desired solution (their own words), tech-savviness, JTBD statement, SunnySideUp feature, grounding fact.

Three personas rather than the two `scope.md` set as the minimum. `scope.md` explicitly left open whether the "second opinion" use-timing warranted its own persona, to be decided once research was in. It does: the CMA's pricing-opacity finding, the quote-complexity research, and the Energy Saving Trust competitive gap are among the most specific, differentiated findings in the whole document, and they describe a distinct moment (holding an actual quote) rather than the same proactive research behavior as persona 1. Splitting them avoids flattening SunnySideUp's sharpest differentiator into a single generic "homeowner" persona.

---

## 1. Graham Whitfield — The Sales-Call Avoider
**Age/gender:** 58, male · **Location:** Solihull, West Midlands · **Occupation:** Project manager (semi-retired, works part-time) · **Household:** Homeowner, married, empty-nester · **Income:** ~£52k (household)

> "I don't want a salesman doing my sums for me before I've even worked out if this makes sense."

**Goal:** Get an honest, no-pressure read on whether rooftop solar makes financial sense for his house before he ever speaks to an installer.
**Pain point:** Every "free quote" comparison site he's tried online wants his phone number before showing him anything useful, and a neighbour's experience with the calls that followed put him off picking up the phone himself.
**Current behavior:** Read about the government's 2025 Solar Roadmap and got interested, but has stalled for months specifically to avoid entering the sales funnel before he has his own sense of the numbers.
**Desired solution (his words):** "Just tell me, based on my actual roof and my actual bills, whether this is worth doing. I'll call an installer myself once I know."
**Tech-savviness:** Moderate. Comfortable filling in a web form; wouldn't want to install anything or read a spreadsheet.
**JTBD:** When I'm considering solar for the first time, I want an independent read on my specific financial case before any installer contacts me, so I can enter that conversation informed instead of exposed.
**SunnySideUp feature:** Pre-conversation viability check (postcode plus a few details, before any installer contact).
**Grounding fact:** Every UK solar comparison platform identified in research runs a lead-generation or referral-fee model, paid per lead rather than for helping a homeowner decide; the UK government's 2025 Solar Roadmap targets 45–47GW of installed capacity by 2030, prioritizing rooftop, which is the kind of thing that gets someone like Graham looking in the first place.

## 2. Denise Okafor — The Quote Skeptic
**Age/gender:** 47, female · **Location:** Reading, Berkshire · **Occupation:** HR manager · **Household:** Homeowner, married, two teenagers · **Income:** ~£68k (combined)

> "Two companies, two numbers four thousand pounds apart, and neither one explains why."

**Goal:** Work out whether the solar quote she just received is actually fair before she signs anything.
**Pain point:** After entering her postcode on a comparison site, she started getting cold calls, one including a claim about a "government-required" safety check she later couldn't find any record of. Two quotes she did request for what she was told was "the same system" differed by thousands of pounds with no breakdown of what was actually included in each.
**Current behavior:** Searching the installer's name plus "reviews" and asking in a local Facebook group, neither of which tells her whether her specific numbers make sense.
**Desired solution (her words):** "Just tell me if this quote is in the right ballpark for my house, and what it's actually including, before I sign anything."
**Tech-savviness:** Moderate.
**JTBD:** When I've just received a solar quote, I want an independent check on whether the price and system size are reasonable for my situation, so I don't overpay or get talked into add-ons I don't need.
**SunnySideUp feature:** Post-quote second-opinion check, the same viability engine run against a specific quote rather than a blank-slate question.
**Grounding fact:** The CMA's 2022 investigation into this sector specifically flagged "inaccurate and unrealistic headline price information," and a Which? survey found more than a third of solar-panel owners had been cold-called with false claims, including mandatory "government-required" checks, both regulator/consumer-body findings. Separately, multiple independent solar-advice sources consistently describe installers varying system-size definitions, included services, and financing terms enough that quotes for a similar job can differ by thousands of pounds without an obvious reason why. That pattern recurs widely but isn't confirmed by an authoritative body the way the CMA and Which? findings are.

## 3. Aisha Rahman — The Plug-In Fence-Sitter
**Age/gender:** 29, female · **Location:** Manchester (private rented flat) · **Occupation:** Graphic designer, hybrid · **Household:** Private renter, lives with a partner · **Income:** ~£31k

> "Everyone online says I can just plug it in. No two of them agree on whether that's actually still true."

**Goal:** Find out whether she's actually allowed to install a plug-in solar kit right now, and whether it's worth the money if so.
**Pain point:** Has a plug-in solar kit sitting in an online shopping basket but hasn't bought it. Every site selling one claims installation is simple and that landlords have no say, but she can't tell whether that's accurate or just a sales line, and the legal status keeps shifting under her (electrician-installed is one thing, DIY self-install is another, and the dates don't match across sources).
**Current behavior:** Has read several blog posts and a government consultation summary trying to piece together the actual legal picture herself, and still isn't confident she has it right.
**Desired solution (her words):** "Just tell me straight: can I actually do this in my flat right now, legally, and is it even worth the money?"
**Tech-savviness:** High. Comfortable doing her own research online; frustrated that careful searching still hasn't produced a clear, trustworthy answer.
**JTBD:** When I'm considering a plug-in solar kit as a renter, I want a clear, unbiased read on both legality and value for my specific situation, so I don't waste money or end up in a dispute with my landlord.
**SunnySideUp feature:** Plug-in viability and legal-status check, including an explicit "this part is still genuinely unresolved" flag where the underlying research itself hasn't settled the answer.
**Grounding fact:** BS 7671 Amendment 4 legalized electrician-installed plug-in solar from 15 April 2026; true DIY self-install becomes legal 27 August 2026 under SI 2026/848, not before; the specific claim that renters need zero landlord permission for a plug-in kit has no support in the Renters' Rights Act 2025's actual text and traces only to sites with a commercial incentive to say so, a genuinely open question this persona's own uncertainty mirrors rather than one SunnySideUp can fully resolve for her today.
