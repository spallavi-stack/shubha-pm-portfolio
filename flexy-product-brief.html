Flexy - Product Brief

One-liner

Flexy is a plug-in companion to your utility's app that turns a dynamic tariff plus time-of-use pricing - too many variables for a regular person to track - into simple, automatic savings: cheaper EV charging and clear price and usage visibility.

Positioning statement

For ComEd customers on a real-time or time-of-use rate, Flexy is the plug-in that watches the price on their behalf - showing what electricity costs right now and over time, and automating EV charging into the cheapest hours. Flexy is not a utility and does not compete with one; it's a white-label layer a utility can hand to its own customers. ComEd is the first pilot client, not the product's only home; Flexy is built to plug into any utility in any state offering a comparable dynamic or TOU tariff.

Why Illinois, and why now

Illinois is the strongest available anchor for a true dynamic-tariff story in the US. ComEd (and Ameren Illinois) run a genuine hourly Residential Real-Time Pricing program - supply rates track the actual wholesale market price hour by hour, not a fixed morning/evening split - and ComEd is finalizing a rollout of time-of-use rates in 2026 after a four-year pilot that cut participants' peak demand 6.5-9.7% each summer. That's the closest US equivalent to what Octopus Agile does in the UK or Tibber does in the Nordics: a tariff volatile and complex enough that a regular household genuinely cannot track it unaided, which is exactly the gap Flexy is built to close.

This sits inside the broader 2025-2026 US shift covered earlier in this brief: the federal solar tax credit and heat pump credits expired December 31, 2025, net metering is now the main driver of solar ROI and varies state by state, and average electric bills are up 26% in five years. Illinois gives us a concrete, well-documented tariff to build against, while the underlying bill-shock, solar-ROI, and heat-pump-decision problems are the same ones facing households nationally.

MVP scope: single utility client (ComEd), single state (Illinois), homeowners on - or eligible for - ComEd's real-time or time-of-use rate. Other utilities and states (any offering a comparable smart tariff) are the natural expansion path, treated as a client-acquisition and rollout question on the roadmap rather than built into the MVP.

Business model: B2B2C. Flexy's paying/partner relationship is with the utility (ComEd first), which distributes or recommends Flexy to its rate-plan customers; the end user is the ComEd customer and never pays directly. The utility pays two ways: a fee per customer registered through the rollout, plus an annual license fee for the Flexy platform itself. This is a deliberate structural choice - it's the same model WeaveGrid and Optiwatt use with US utilities, and it avoids Flexy having to acquire individual households cold.

Future expansion (roadmap-level, not MVP): other US utilities with dynamic/TOU tariffs, and eventually the pan-European vision (UK, Netherlands, and other EU markets) discussed earlier in this brief, sequenced later as explicit "market/client expansion" workstreams.

Core features (MVP)

  1. Price information - price graph views for yesterday, today, tomorrow, plus monthly-average and annual views, so users can see both the immediate decision window and the longer trend.
  2. Real-time consumption - live household usage pulled from the smart meter / utility data feed. Informational only in the MVP - Flexy does not attempt to control other household devices (dishwasher, thermostat, etc.); that would require per-appliance smart-plug/API integrations well beyond MVP scope. EV charging is the one device category Flexy actually automates, because EV charging APIs (Smartcar, ChargePoint, automaker APIs) already exist and are what Optiwatt/WeaveGrid build on - general appliance control is a possible later roadmap item, not an MVP promise.
  3. Smart EV charging - connect one or more EVs; Flexy charges automatically in the cheapest available hours against the live tariff, with user-set preferences (e.g. "charge by 8am tomorrow," minimum charge level, cost-vs-readiness priority - patterned on how Octopus/Ohme and Optiwatt let users balance savings against having enough range when they need it).

Solar and heat pump savings calculators were cut from MVP scope; see Status below.

Core problems Flexy addresses

  1. Bill shock & rate distrust - households don't understand what they're paying or why; average bills up 26% in five years, with continued rate increases into 2026.
  2. Smart meter data going unused - ~70-73% of US residential meters are already smart, but the data rarely turns into action.
  3. EV home charging cost/scheduling - people charge at peak-price hours by accident, missing overnight TOU windows that can be 30-60% cheaper.

Household journey (the narrative spine)

  1. Understand what you're paying → price graph (yesterday/today/tomorrow/monthly/annual)
  2. Understand your usage → real-time consumption view
  3. Optimize day-to-day → smart EV charging against the live tariff

Each portfolio mini-project (research, roadmap, prototype, PMF analysis, funnel analysis, AI-assisted feedback synthesis) attaches to this same journey, product, and set of personas - so the portfolio reads as one coherent product story rather than a set of disconnected exercises.

Personas

Four personas, all tied directly to the EV-charging and price-visibility MVP (see flexy-personas.md): the Accidental Peak Charger, the TOU Skeptic, the Overwhelmed Watcher, and the Multi-EV Household.

Competitive analysis

Direct inspiration (EU) - the "Tibber model" Flexy is adapting for a US, utility-partnered context: - Tibber (Norway, Sweden, Germany, Netherlands) - sells dynamic-price electricity directly plus the Tibber Pulse device for real-time consumption and app-based smart EV charging. Closest functional model to Flexy's price + consumption + EV charging combination, but Tibber is the retail energy supplier - a B2C model, not a plug-in to someone else's utility. - Octopus Energy / Kraken platform (UK) - Intelligent Octopus Go auto-schedules EV charging within a guaranteed cheap overnight window; customers on it saved an average of £771/year (May 2025-April 2026) vs. the standard variable tariff. Again, Octopus is the supplier, not a third-party layer. - Ohme (UK) - EV charging hardware + app that works across suppliers (not tied to one utility), scheduling charging around whichever tariff the user is on. Structurally closer to Flexy's "plug-in, not the utility" model, but scoped to EV charging only - no price-graph or consumption features. - Jedlix (Netherlands) - B2B2C smart EV charging platform that partners with automakers and utilities/suppliers, optimizing charging against dynamic tariffs like Octopus Agile, Tibber, and Frank Energie. Structurally the closest match to Flexy's business model (utility/OEM partner, not the retail brand) - but again, EV charging only, no broader household energy decision support.

US market - where Flexy would actually compete or partner: - Optiwatt - consumer app and "energy management platform for utilities," integrates a household's exact utility rate plan to auto-schedule EV charging and HVAC for the cheapest hours (avg. claimed savings ~$388/year); explicitly offers a utility-partnership/white-label model, making it the closest direct US competitor to Flexy's plug-in positioning. Gap: no combined price-graph + consumption view surfaced in its consumer offering. - WeaveGrid - partners directly with utilities (e.g. PG&E, BGE) for managed EV charging: real-time charge status, rate information, optimal charging windows, utility program/rewards enrollment (e.g. ChargePerks in California). Very close to Flexy's B2B2C-with-a-utility model. Gap: focused on EV charging and utility program enrollment, not price-graph/consumption visualization. - Emporia - free app plus hardware (Vue energy monitor, EV chargers, smart plugs) that shows real-time circuit-level consumption, schedules devices to the cheapest utility rate windows or to match solar production, and includes an AI assistant for usage questions. Closest US competitor on the consumption side. Gap: requires buying hardware; not positioned as a utility-distributed plug-in, and EV smart charging is tied to owning an Emporia charger specifically. - Span / Sense - Span is a premium smart electrical panel (hardware-heavy, $3,500-5,000 installed) aimed at solar/battery/EV households already renovating. Sense discontinued consumer hardware sales at the end of 2025 and is pivoting toward licensing its software directly to utilities - itself a signal that the "utility-distributed software layer" model Flexy is proposing is where this market is heading.

What app store reviews reveal, beyond the marketing pages: marketing copy describes what these products claim; real reviews show what breaks once someone actually relies on them. Optiwatt's own App Store reviews surface a recurring trust failure: cars charging during peak hours despite a saved schedule, exactly the kind of automation-trust risk Flexy is betting against. Ohme's reviews show a different gap: when the API can't read a vehicle's battery, there's no simple time-based fallback, so the app stops working for that user entirely. Tibber's reviews flag a related honesty problem: the app shows only the commodity rate, leaving out grid and demand fees that can make up most of the real bill. None of this shows up on a marketing page - it's what real users hit once they're actually depending on the product day to day.

The gap Flexy targets: no single US player currently combines (a) live dynamic/TOU price visibility, (b) real-time consumption, and (c) hardware-agnostic smart EV charging, delivered as a plug-in a utility can hand to its own customers rather than a separate hardware purchase or a competing energy supplier. Optiwatt and WeaveGrid validate the business model; Tibber, Jedlix, and Emporia validate pieces of the feature set. Flexy's bet is that stitching all three into one utility-distributed layer is the differentiated position.

Measuring product-market fit

Since Flexy is B2B2C, PMF isn't one signal - it's two: does the utility keep paying, and do the households it enrolls actually use the product. Three concrete signals, in order of how directly each one answers that:

  1. Pilot extension. Does ComEd renew or expand the rollout past the initial pilot cohort. This is the clearest signal of all, since it means the utility itself, not just Flexy, sees enough value to keep paying.
  2. Downloads. How many enrolled customers actually install the app once ComEd distributes or recommends it. This tests whether the utility's own distribution channel converts, the first real funnel step on the household side.
  3. Smart charging activations. How many of those downloads go on to actually connect an EV and turn on smart charging, rather than just checking pricing. This is the harder, more meaningful signal, since it requires the most trust (handing over charging control) and ties directly to the app-store-surfaced trust risk named above.

Status

Draft v4 - MVP re-scoped to a single utility client (ComEd) and state (Illinois), assuming a dynamic + TOU tariff; Flexy repositioned as a B2B2C, white-label plug-in rather than a standalone utility or app, with ComEd as the first pilot client rather than the only market. Core feature set defined (price graph, real-time consumption, smart EV charging with user preferences). Solar and heat-pump savings calculators, and the two personas built around them, were cut from scope - outside a realistic MVP and not core to the EV-charging + price-visibility story. Competitive analysis added (EU: Tibber, Octopus/Kraken, Ohme, Jedlix; US: Optiwatt, WeaveGrid, Emporia, Span/Sense), including App Store review findings for Optiwatt, Ohme, and Tibber. Personas built from real ComEd rate and program data - see flexy-personas.md. Business model now specifies a per-registered-customer fee plus an annual platform license fee, and PMF is defined against three concrete signals: pilot extension, downloads, and smart charging activations.
