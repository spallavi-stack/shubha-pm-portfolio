Problem & positioning

The market signal

  • Average US electric bills are up 26% in five years.
  • ~70-73% of US residential meters are already smart, but that data rarely turns into action.
  • The federal solar tax credit and heat pump credits expired December 31, 2025, so net metering and state programs now carry the ROI case alone.
  • ComEd runs a genuine hourly Real-Time Pricing program today and is finalizing a Time-of-Use rate for 2026, after a pilot that cut peak demand 6.5-9.7% each summer.

Positioning

Flexy is not a utility and doesn't compete with one. It's a white-label plug-in layer a utility can hand to its own customers (B2B2C), the same model Optiwatt and WeaveGrid already use in the US. ComEd/Illinois is the first pilot market, not the only one — it's the closest US equivalent to Octopus Agile in the UK or Tibber in the Nordics: a tariff volatile enough that no household can track it by hand, and a template for any utility running a comparable dynamic or TOU rate.

What synthetic interviews surfaced

All four personas were interviewed with the same 7-question guide (AI-generated, in-character, grounded strictly in each persona's brief and real ComEd/Illinois data, explicitly flagged as synthetic, not real user data). Three cross-cutting themes emerged and directly shaped the roadmap:

01

Transparency is required, even when automation is welcomed

Every persona who liked EV automation still wanted visibility into why a decision was made, not a black box.

02

Readiness beats savings as the default

The trust-killer isn't a missed discount, it's the car not being ready. Readiness had to outrank maximum savings by default.

03

Willingness to pay splits along B2B2C lines

Highest-pain personas would pay directly; others only if ComEd bundles it for free. This validated the B2B2C model.

A product decision worth showing

The two EV charging modes originally differed by whether a deadline existed at all. That was wrong: every persona interview showed a household always knows when it needs the car. I corrected both modes to require a ready-by time, and redefined the real difference as depth of configuration, full manual control (Custom settings) versus minimal input with sensible defaults (Max savings), validated directly against Marcus and Jenna & Sam (who wanted control) versus Devon (who explicitly wanted the opposite).

Limitations

Industry commentary on AI-assisted prototypes in 2025-2026 converges on one point: a polished demo and a shippable product are different things, and it's easy to mistake one for the other. A few of the gaps commonly named by product practitioners, applied honestly to Flexy:

A working demo isn't 10% of the way to done

  • Stakeholders often see a polished AI-built demo and assume it's nearly finished, when the actual engineering (backend, auth, data persistence, real integrations) hasn't started.
  • Flexy's Price & Cost screen now pulls real, live prices straight from ComEd's public Hourly Pricing API — but that's the one piece that's real. There's still no real backend, no ComEd account login, and no live Smartcar/EV API, so EV charging control and account data remain simulated.
Source: Kyros, "From Prototype to Production"

Automated design still needs real users

  • AI-generated screens can miss subtle hierarchy, spacing, and comprehension issues that only surface with real usability testing.
  • Flexy's personas and interviews are synthetic and internally consistent, but were never tested against an actual human clicking through the flow.
Source: Parallel, "AI-Powered Prototyping for UI/UX Design"

Happy-path only, no edge cases

  • AI-built prototypes handle the intended flow well but typically skip error states, failed API calls, and concurrent-use conditions.
  • Flexy's click-dummy shows the ideal path only — no built-out states for a failed ComEd login, a stale price feed, or a car that drops connection mid-session.
Source: CodeConductor, "Vibe Coding for PMs"

Prototype code isn't production code

  • AI-generated prototypes are typically isolated from any real codebase and would need to be re-implemented by an engineering team to actually ship.
  • Flexy's click-dummy is static HTML/CSS/JS with no data layer, no security review, and no compliance pass — relevant since a real version would handle utility account data and OAuth tokens to EV manufacturer APIs.
Source: monday.com, "Vibe coding for product managers"
