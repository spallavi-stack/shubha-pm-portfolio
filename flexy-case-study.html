60-second version

The short version

US electricity bills are up 26% in five years, and households on dynamic or time-of-use rates have no real way to track hour-by-hour prices themselves. Flexy is a B2B2C companion app that a utility (starting with ComEd in Illinois) hands to its own customers: it shows what electricity costs right now, automatically shifts EV charging into the cheapest hours, and gives honest payback numbers on solar and heat pumps before a homeowner talks to a contractor. I scoped the MVP down to what's actually buildable (EV charging is the only device Flexy automates; everything else is informational or advisory), grounded every persona and roadmap decision in real Illinois program data and real competitor behavior, and built a working phone-frame click-dummy of the full flow.

Problem & market

Why Illinois, and why now

Dynamic and time-of-use electricity pricing is spreading across the US, but it only saves people money if they can act on it in real time, something almost no household actually does unaided.

The market signal

  • Average US electric bills are up 26% in five years.
  • ~70-73% of US residential meters are already smart, but that data rarely turns into action.
  • The federal solar tax credit and heat pump credits expired December 31, 2025, so net metering and state programs now carry the ROI case alone.
  • ComEd runs a genuine hourly Real-Time Pricing program today and is finalizing a Time-of-Use rate for 2026, after a pilot that cut peak demand 6.5-9.7% each summer.

Positioning

Flexy is not a utility and doesn't compete with one. It's a plug-in layer on top of a utility's existing smart-tariff program, distributed by the utility to its own customers (B2B2C), the same model Optiwatt and WeaveGrid already use in the US. ComEd/Illinois is the launch market because it's the closest US equivalent to Octopus Agile in the UK or Tibber in the Nordics: a tariff volatile enough that no household can track it by hand.

What synthetic interviews surfaced

All six personas were interviewed with the same 7-question guide (AI-generated, in-character, grounded strictly in each persona's brief and real ComEd/Illinois data, explicitly flagged as synthetic, not real user data). Four cross-cutting themes emerged and directly shaped the roadmap:

01

Transparency is required, even when automation is welcomed

Every persona who liked EV automation still wanted visibility into why a decision was made, not a black box.

02

Readiness beats savings as the default

The trust-killer isn't a missed discount, it's the car not being ready. Readiness had to outrank maximum savings by default.

03

Willingness to pay splits along B2B2C lines

Highest-pain and highest-stakes personas would pay directly; others only if ComEd bundles it for free. This validated the B2B2C model.

04

Trust bar rises with financial stakes

Daily automation (EV charging) earns trust by "just working." Five-figure decisions (solar, heat pump) require showing assumptions and independence from any contractor.

Prioritization

ICE-prioritized roadmap

Scored on Impact, Confidence, and Ease rather than RICE, since Reach isn't meaningfully estimable pre-launch with no real user base. Every score is tied back to a specific persona, JTBD, or interview theme, not a gut call. The roadmap is dependency-aware, not just sorted by score: the two solar/heat-pump calculators score respectably but sit in Next because they depend on infrastructure Now builds first.

A product decision worth showing

The two EV charging modes originally differed by whether a deadline existed at all. That was wrong: every persona interview showed a household always knows when it needs the car. I corrected both modes to require a ready-by time, and redefined the real difference as depth of configuration, full manual control (Custom settings) versus minimal input with sensible defaults (Max savings), validated directly against Marcus and Jenna & Sam (who wanted control) versus Devon (who explicitly wanted the opposite).

Reflection

What this project demonstrates

Product judgment

  • Scoped an MVP down to what's technically honest (EV charging only) instead of overpromising full home automation.
  • Caught and fixed a real prioritization flaw (Max Savings mode having no deadline) using interview evidence.
  • Chose ICE over RICE deliberately, and explained why, rather than defaulting to a familiar framework.
  • Grounded a fictional product in real, current program data (ComEd RTP/TOU filings, Illinois Shines, HEAR) instead of invented numbers.

Working with AI as a collaborator

  • Used AI for research synthesis, synthetic interviews, and rapid prototyping, while directing every scoping and prioritization call myself.
  • Iteratively critiqued and corrected AI output: reworked the energy-flow diagram topology, fixed stale UI copy, and rescoped screens after walking through the flow myself.
  • Kept synthetic research honestly labeled throughout rather than presenting it as real user data.

Open next step

The Analytics/Price & Cost screen is mid-rescope: it currently overlaps with the new Home screen and needs its own pass before the prototype is fully polished, a known, named gap rather than a hidden one.
